# HPV Vaccine Q&A — India (MoHFW / WHO / ICMR)

## What is HPV?

Human Papillomavirus (HPV) is a common sexually transmitted virus. There are more than 200 types of HPV; some cause genital warts and others can lead to cancers of the cervix, vagina, vulva, penis, anus, and oropharynx.

## What is the HPV vaccine?

The HPV vaccine protects against the most dangerous strains of HPV. In India, the following vaccines are approved:

- **Gardasil** (quadrivalent) – protects against HPV types 6, 11, 16, and 18.
- **Cervarix** (bivalent) – protects against HPV types 16 and 18.
- **Gardasil 9** (nine-valent) – protects against HPV types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, and 58.
- **Cervavac** – India's first indigenously developed quadrivalent HPV vaccine, approved by CDSCO.

## Who should get the HPV vaccine in India?

According to MoHFW and ICMR guidelines:

- **Primary target group**: Girls aged **9–14 years** (before sexual debut) — highest efficacy.
- **Secondary target group**: Girls and women aged **15–26 years** who have not yet been vaccinated.
- **Boys and men**: HPV vaccination is also beneficial for males to prevent HPV-related cancers (penile, anal, oropharyngeal) and genital warts. Recommended for ages 9–26 years.
- Adults **above 26 years** may benefit; consult a doctor.

## How many doses are required?

- **9–14 years**: **2 doses** — second dose given 6 months after the first.
- **15 years and above, or immunocompromised**: **3 doses** — at 0, 1–2, and 6 months.

## When should the HPV vaccine be given?

The vaccine is most effective when given **before any exposure to HPV**, ideally before the start of sexual activity. However, it still provides benefit even after sexual activity has begun.

## Is the HPV vaccine safe?

Yes. HPV vaccines have been extensively studied in clinical trials and post-market surveillance involving millions of doses worldwide. Common side effects are mild:

- Pain, redness, or swelling at the injection site
- Mild fever
- Headache or dizziness

Serious adverse events are extremely rare. The vaccine is endorsed by WHO, the Indian Academy of Pediatrics (IAP), and MoHFW.

## Can the HPV vaccine prevent cervical cancer?

Yes. HPV types 16 and 18 together cause approximately **70% of cervical cancer cases** in India. Vaccination against these types, combined with regular cervical cancer screening (Pap smear or HPV DNA test), significantly reduces the risk.

## Does vaccination eliminate the need for cervical cancer screening?

**No.** Vaccination does not replace routine screening. Women who are vaccinated should continue to undergo regular cervical cancer screening as recommended by their healthcare provider.

## Is the HPV vaccine available in the Indian government immunisation programme?

The Government of India introduced the HPV vaccine into the **Universal Immunisation Programme (UIP)** for girls aged 9–14 years as a two-dose schedule. The indigenously developed **Cervavac** vaccine is being rolled out under this programme.

## Can pregnant women receive the HPV vaccine?

**No.** The HPV vaccine is not recommended during pregnancy. Women who are pregnant should wait until after delivery. Breastfeeding women may receive the vaccine.

## Can the HPV vaccine treat existing HPV infections or cervical cancer?

**No.** The HPV vaccine is **preventive**, not therapeutic. It does not treat existing infections or cancers.

## What is the cost of the HPV vaccine in India?

- Under the government programme (UIP), the vaccine is available **free of charge** for eligible girls aged 9–14.
- In the private sector, the cost varies by brand and facility. Consult your healthcare provider or local pharmacy for current pricing.

## Is the HPV vaccine halal/acceptable under religious guidelines?

Leading Islamic and other religious bodies have not issued prohibitions against the HPV vaccine. It is a preventive health measure endorsed by public health authorities globally.

## HPV Vaccine and India's Burden of Cervical Cancer

India accounts for approximately **one-quarter of the world's cervical cancer burden**. Cervical cancer is the second most common cancer among Indian women. The HPV vaccine is a key strategy in India's National Cancer Control Programme to reduce this burden.
